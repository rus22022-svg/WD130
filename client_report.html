Client / Developer Team Project Report

Kalob Rust — WDD 130 — 2025

Client Report

How did you decide what content and images to use for your site plan?

I started by thinking about what I actually wanted the site to communicate — my identity as a Cybersecurity student who is also learning web development. I chose an editorial design style because I wanted it to feel clean and professional rather than generic. For content I leaned on things that were true to me: my background, my interests, and the actual work I completed throughout the course. Images were kept minimal and purposeful, using my own logo mark and personal photo rather than stock imagery.

How did you communicate with your developer? Was there any issues with communication?

I communicated with my developer primarily through Email and Canvas. Both tools worked well enough for exchanging files and feedback, though async communication always introduces some delay. There were no major breakdowns, but more real-time communication would have helped catch misalignments sooner.

Did your developer meet your expectations for your site?

Not fully. The site they built didn't follow my site plan very closely and didn't match my design expectations as well as I had hoped. That said, the end result was still a functional website and overall a decent effort — just not quite what I had envisioned. It reinforced how important clear, detailed communication and a thorough site plan really are.

What will you do differently next time you hire a team to do a project for you?

I would put significantly more detail into my site plan — more specific wireframes, color hex codes clearly labeled, and annotated notes on layout decisions. I would also follow up more frequently over Email and Canvas rather than waiting for a finished product, so I could catch deviations from the plan earlier in the process.

Lead Developer Report

How did you manage your development team and communicate the tasks for your junior devs?

I communicated task assignments through Email and Canvas. I outlined which pages each junior developer was responsible for and shared the repository link so they could access the codebase. I tried to be clear about what pages needed to be built and pointed them toward the client's site plan for design guidance.

How did you communicate with your client? Was there any issues?

All communication with the client was done through Email and Canvas. There were no major issues — the client was responsive and I shared the published site link with them so they could review progress. Async communication worked reasonably well for this type of project.

How did you handle publishing your client's site and sharing it with the client?

I published the site using GitHub Pages and shared the live URL with the client through Email and Canvas so they could review it at any time. This made it easy for the client to check progress without needing to understand anything about Git or repositories.

How was your experience with managing your site with GitHub?

Managing the repository was one of the more challenging aspects. Setting up the repo, giving junior developers access, and keeping everything organized took some trial and error. By the end of the project I felt much more comfortable with GitHub as a collaboration tool.

Junior Developer Report

How did you communicate with your team lead? Was there any issues?

Communication varied significantly between teams. One team lead never contacted me at all — I had no direction, no access details, and no communication throughout the project. The other team went smoothly, with clear instructions delivered through Email and Canvas and enough guidance to complete my assigned page without issues.

Did you know what was expected of you for each site?

For the team where communication worked, yes — I had a clear understanding of what page I was building and what design standards to follow. For the team where the lead never reached out, I genuinely did not know what was expected because there was no communication to go off of. That was a frustrating experience and highlighted how critical the lead developer role really is.

Were you able to contribute to your sites?

For the team with good communication, yes — I was able to complete my assigned child page and contribute meaningfully to the project. For the team where I never heard from the lead, my ability to contribute was effectively blocked by the lack of contact and access.

Did you run into any issues with GitHub and adding your changes to the sites?

Nothing major on the technical side for the team where things ran smoothly. The bigger issue was simply not having repository access for the team where communication broke down entirely, which made GitHub a non-issue by default since I could never get in to begin with.

General Experience and Reflections

What was the most challenging part of this project for you? Were you able to overcome it?

The most challenging part was wearing three different hats — client, lead developer, and junior developer — simultaneously. Switching mindsets between "what do I want as a client" and "how do I build this as a developer" took deliberate effort. I overcame it by treating each role separately and giving each one focused attention rather than trying to blur them together.

What did you enjoy most about this project?

Seeing my own portfolio come together as a real, published website was genuinely rewarding. It is one thing to complete assignments; it is another to have something online with your name on it that you built from scratch. The collaborative aspect was also enjoyable — seeing how other students approached the same design problems differently was interesting and taught me things I would not have learned working alone.

What would you do differently if you could do the project again?

I would invest more time in the planning phase — more detailed wireframes, a more thorough site plan, and earlier communication with my developer. I would also start using Git branches properly from day one rather than learning the hard way mid-project. Planning and version control are things that feel like overhead until you realize how much time they save.

Do you feel more confident in working on real-world projects after this experience? Explain.

Yes, meaningfully so. Before this project, "working on a team" in a development context was abstract to me. Now I have a concrete sense of what that workflow looks like — how clients communicate with developers, how lead developers manage a team and a repository, and how junior developers contribute without breaking what already exists. That hands-on experience, even at a small scale, translates directly to how real development teams operate.
